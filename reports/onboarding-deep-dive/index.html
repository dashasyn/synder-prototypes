🔐 Sign In Page

Page Structure

  • Synder logo
  • 3 SSO buttons (horizontal row): Intuit (filled blue), Xero (outlined), Google (outlined w/ colored border)
  • No "or" divider
  • Email field + Password field
  • "Forgot password?" link
  • "Sign in" button
  • "Do not have an account? Sign up" link

Findings

Finding S1: SSO vs Email — no visual hierarchy High

Evidence: All 4 sign-up buttons have identical visual weight (outlined, same size). But "Sign in with Intuit" and "Continue with Xero" are SSO flows that skip password creation entirely, while "Continue with Email" leads to a manual form — a fundamentally different action.

Recommendation: Group SSO buttons visually (e.g., labeled section "Quick sign up with:") and separate from the email path with an "— or —" divider. Consider making SSO buttons primary and email secondary to nudge users toward the faster path.

Finding S2: Verb inconsistency — "Sign in" on a sign-up page High

Evidence: On the Sign Up page: "Sign in with Intuit" vs "Continue with Xero/Google/Email". One button says "sign in," three say "continue." On the Sign In page: "Sign in with Intuit/Xero/Google" — different verb from sign-up page.

Recommendation: Use one consistent verb per page. Sign Up page → "Sign up with Intuit / Continue with Xero / ..." Sign In page → "Sign in with Intuit / Sign in with Xero / ..." Never mix "sign in" and "continue" on the same screen.

Finding S3: SSO button styling differs completely between pages High

Evidence: Sign Up: vertical stack, all outlined buttons with consistent style. Sign In: horizontal row, Intuit = filled blue, Xero = outlined, Google = outlined with colored border. These are the same auth actions on sibling pages but look completely different. A user toggling between them may wonder if they're on the same product.

Recommendation: Use identical button styling for SSO across both pages. Same layout (vertical or horizontal), same colors, same sizing. The only difference should be the page heading and primary CTA text.

Finding S4: Pre-checked promotions checkbox Medium

Evidence: "Receive special promotions and offers" is pre-checked on the Sign Up page. Depending on jurisdiction (GDPR, CAN-SPAM), this may be a compliance risk. Even where legal, pre-checked opt-ins erode trust — users who notice feel opted-in without consent.

Recommendation: Default to unchecked. Users who want promotions will check it voluntarily — and they'll be higher-quality subscribers. For GDPR regions, pre-checked consent boxes may not meet "freely given" consent requirements.

Finding S5: No "or" divider between SSO and email login Medium

Evidence: On the Sign In page, the 3 SSO buttons end and email/password fields begin with no visual separator. Most SaaS products use an "— or —" line to clearly separate alternative auth paths. Without it, users must mentally parse that these are alternative methods, not sequential steps.

Recommendation: Add a standard "— or sign in with email —" divider between SSO buttons and the email form. This is an established pattern (Google, Slack, Notion, Linear all use it).

Finding S6: Two visual containers on sign-in — unclear separation Medium

Evidence: The Sign In page splits SSO buttons and email/password into what appears to be two separate card/box areas. This creates a visual split that makes the page feel like two separate forms rather than "pick your method."

Recommendation: Use a single container with an "or" divider inside. One card = one purpose = one mental model for the user.

Finding S7: Right panel value props are generic Low

Evidence: "Advanced sales data tracking" and "Detailed financial reporting" could describe any accounting tool. These don't differentiate Synder from QuickBooks, Xero, or FreshBooks. The core value prop — automated multi-channel sync from 30+ platforms — isn't mentioned.

Recommendation: Replace generic bullets with Synder-specific differentiators: "Auto-sync from Stripe, Shopify, Amazon & 30+ platforms", "Reconcile in minutes, not hours", "Multi-channel sales in one dashboard". Show what users CAN'T get elsewhere.

Finding S8: "Join thousands" vs "3,400+ reviews" — vague meets specific Low

Evidence: "Join thousands of users" sits alongside "3,400+ reviews" with a star rating. The vague "thousands" undermines the specific "3,400+" — if you have a real number, use it.

Recommendation: Replace "Join thousands of users" with a specific claim: "Trusted by 10,000+ businesses" (or whatever the real number is). Specificity builds credibility.

Finding S9: "No credit card required" appears twice Low

Evidence: "No credit card required" shows as a trust badge at the top AND as a bullet point in the right panel's trial description. Redundancy in a compact space wastes attention.

Recommendation: Keep it in one prominent location — the trust badge row is the stronger placement. Remove from the bullet list and replace with a unique benefit.

Finding S10: "Do not have an account?" — stiff phrasing Low

Evidence: Sign In page uses "Do not have an account?" — grammatically correct but unnaturally formal. Every major SaaS uses the contraction: "Don't have an account?"

Recommendation: Change to "Don't have an account? Sign up". Small copy fix, big normalcy signal.

Finding S11: Password field — unclear visibility toggle Low

Evidence: The password field shows a •••-style icon that may or may not be a show/hide toggle. Standard pattern is an eye icon (👁) that clearly communicates "click to reveal password."

Recommendation: Use a standard eye/eye-off icon with a tooltip or aria-label "Show password" / "Hide password." Ensure it actually toggles visibility.

Drop-off Reasons by Step

Critical Step 1: Role Selection — 63.9% abandon

The #1 problem. Nearly two-thirds of all users who reach onboarding never get past the role selector.

Probable reasons:

  • Opaque label: "How would you describe your role?" gives zero context about WHY the role matters or what changes based on selection
  • Dropdown hides options: Users can't see all choices without clicking — unlike card-based selectors that show everything at a glance
  • Decision paralysis: 6 options, unclear which to pick. "IT & Technology department" and "Marketing" feel wrong for most ecommerce users
  • No "skip" or "I'll decide later": Required field with no way to proceed without choosing
  • Context switch: Users just signed up with marketing promises → land on a blank page with one dropdown and zero context

High Step 2: Business/Client Details — ~30% drop between steps 1 and 2

Probable reasons:

  • Too many required fields: 6-7 required fields on one screen feels heavy
  • Hidden conditional field: Revenue question appears AFTER filling other fields — feels like a moving goalpost
  • Industry multi-select confusion: "Please select all that apply" but the select component doesn't look like a multi-select — it looks like a standard dropdown
  • Silent form failures: Clicking "Next step" with empty fields doesn't scroll to the first error — validation messages appear but may be off-screen
  • Accountant invite pressure: Being asked to invite an accountant mid-setup can feel premature or irrelevant

High Step 3: Integration Selection — ~20% drop

Probable reasons:

  • Must select BOTH sales AND accounting integrations: Business owners must select an accounting platform (QuickBooks, Xero, etc.) but these aren't shown in the default view or "View all" modal — silent failure with no error on first click
  • Overwhelming platform list: "View all integrations" shows 30+ options
  • No guidance: Which ones should I connect first? What if I'm not sure?
  • "Choose at least one integration to proceed" — error appears on SECOND click only, doesn't explain what TYPE of integration is needed

Medium Step 4: Connect Integrations — ~25% drop

Probable reasons:

  • OAuth complexity: Connecting to Stripe, PayPal, etc. requires leaving Synder → logging into platform → authorizing
  • Account readiness: Users may not have their platform credentials handy
  • Fear of access: "What exactly will Synder access?" — security concerns
  • Multi-step OAuth: Some platforms (QuickBooks) have multi-step authorization

Findings & Recommendations

8 findings prioritized by impact. Each includes severity, evidence, and a specific recommendation.

Finding #1: Role selection kills 64% of users Critical

Evidence: 1,101 sessions reach onboarding; only 397 proceed past role selection. The dropdown label "Choose an option" (or "How would you describe your role?") provides zero context.

Recommendation: Replace dropdown with visual card-based selector showing 3-4 clear options with icons and descriptions: "I run an online business" / "I'm an accountant managing clients" / "Other". Reduce from 6 options to 3 — IT, Marketing, and Staff Accountant get the same flow as Business Owner anyway.

Finding #2: Hidden required field — Revenue appears conditionally Critical

Evidence: On the Business Owner flow, "Your revenue for the last year" only appears after filling Industry and Duration. Users who fill the visible fields and click "Next step" get blocked by a field they didn't even know existed. No scroll-to-error behavior.

Recommendation: Either (a) show the Revenue field from the start, or (b) make it optional. If it must be conditional, smoothly animate it into view with a gentle highlight so users notice the new field.

Finding #3: No progress indication or context Critical

Evidence: The text tabs ("Tell us about you → Provide business details → Select integrations → Connect integrations") are flat, hard to scan, and don't show completion state. For the Accounting Firm flow, there are 5 actual screens but only 4 tabs — the Practice Info sub-step is hidden inside Step 1.

Recommendation: Add a numbered progress stepper with visual dots, completion checkmarks, and clear step counts. Show "Step 2 of 4" explicitly. See the Progress Stepper tab for a prototype.

Finding #4: Industry field is a non-standard multi-select High

Evidence: The Industry dropdown uses a custom react-select component with a hidden #clickableInput element. It's a multi-select ("Please select all that apply") but looks identical to single-select dropdowns. Users may not realize they can select multiple industries. The component also loses selections unpredictably in automated testing — suggesting potential state management issues for real users too.

Recommendation: Use checkbox-style multi-select with visible chips/tags for selected items. Or use a standard <select multiple>. Make the multi-select nature visually obvious.

Finding #5: Integration selection has a hidden accounting gate High

Evidence: Business owners must select at least one accounting integration (QuickBooks, Xero, etc.) in addition to sales integrations — but accounting options aren't prominently shown. The error message "Choose at least one integration to proceed" appears only on the second click and doesn't specify WHICH TYPE of integration is needed.

Recommendation: Split Step 3 into two clear sub-sections: "Where do you sell?" (sales platforms) and "Where do you do your books?" (accounting software). Make both visibly required with section labels.

Finding #6: 4 roles with identical flows waste the role selector High

Evidence: Business Owner, Staff Accountant, IT & Technology, and Marketing all lead to the same "Provide business details" flow (with minor variations in the accountant question). Only Accounting Firm and Other get a different path. This means 67% of role options are cosmetic.

Recommendation: Consolidate to 2-3 meaningful paths: "Business owner" / "Accountant/Bookkeeper managing clients" / "Other". Use the role selection to drive genuinely different experiences (welcome copy, feature highlights, default settings). If all paths converge, remove the selector entirely.

Finding #7: Accountant invite creates premature friction Medium

Evidence: Business owners are asked "Do you have an accountant/bookkeeper?" with a required email field if "Yes". Selecting "Yes" then trying to proceed without an email blocks the form. The "find a trusted accountant here" link (on "No") is a nice idea but mid-onboarding is the wrong time.

Recommendation: Move the accountant invite to post-onboarding settings or a "next steps" checklist. During onboarding, at most show a simple checkbox "I work with an accountant" and handle the invite later.

Finding #8: No welcome screen or post-signup context Medium

Evidence: Users go from signup directly to the role dropdown with no welcome, no "here's what to expect", no estimated time. The marketing site promises "three simple steps" but the onboarding shows 4 tabs with up to 5 actual screens.

Recommendation: Add a welcome interstitial: "Welcome to Synder! Let's set up your account in about 3 minutes. Here's what we'll do: ① Tell us about your business ② Connect your sales channels ③ Link your accounting software." Builds confidence and sets expectations.

Recommendations for Bot-Friendly Onboarding

Priority 1: Add data-testid attributes to all form elements

Every input, select, radio, button, and card should have a unique data-testid attribute. This is the single biggest improvement for automation compatibility.

Examples:

  • data-testid="onboarding-role-select"
  • data-testid="onboarding-industry-select"
  • data-testid="onboarding-integration-stripe"
  • data-testid="onboarding-next-button"

Priority 2: Replace non-standard react-select with accessible components

The Industry multi-select uses a custom react-select that doesn't respond to standard DOM interactions. Replace with a component that supports aria-* attributes and responds to .fill() / .selectOption().

Priority 3: Add URL-based step routing

Each onboarding step should have a distinct URL or query parameter (/onboarding?step=2). This allows bots to navigate directly to specific steps and detect their current position.

Priority 4: Provide an onboarding API

For true bot/agent onboarding at scale, expose a REST API that accepts all onboarding parameters in a single POST request. This bypasses the UI entirely and allows programmatic org setup.

POST /api/v1/onboarding/complete
{
  "role": "business_owner",
  "businessName": "My Company",
  "country": "US",
  "industry": ["retail_ecommerce"],
  "businessDuration": "1_3_years",
  "revenue": "250k_1m",
  "integrations": ["stripe", "quickbooks_online"]
}

Priority 5: Render all form fields immediately

Hidden/conditional fields (Revenue, accountant email) should be rendered in the DOM from the start (even if visually hidden). This allows bots to discover and fill all fields in a single pass.
